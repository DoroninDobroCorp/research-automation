Federal framework: PROTECT Act and CSAM statutes

Section 1466A, enacted by the PROTECT Act of 2003, criminalizes “obscene visual representations of the sexual abuse of children,” including any “photograph, film, video, picture, digital image or picture, computer image or picture, or computer generated image or picture.” The statute makes it a crime to produce, distribute, receive, transport, or possess visual depictions that (1) depict a minor engaging in sexually explicit conduct and are obscene, or (2) depict an image that is or appears to be of a minor engaged in graphic sexual acts and lacks serious value, and it expressly imports penalty ranges from 18 U.S.C. § 2252A(b). Section 1466A(f)(1) and related commentary emphasize that “visual depiction” includes data stored electronically and “computer generated” images, and § 1466A(c) clarifies it is not required that the depicted minor actually exist, which is central for AI‑generated content and purely fictional characters.

Application to AI‑generated and fictional minors

Because § 1466A’s definition of “visual depiction” expressly includes “computer generated image or picture,” prosecutors can treat AI‑generated images the same as other digital or cartoon depictions if they show a minor—or someone who appears to be a minor—engaged in sexually explicit conduct and the material is obscene or lacks serious value. Defense‑bar summaries note that § 1466A is “uniquely broad” and that people are “often surprised” that “fictional or digitally created images can still lead to federal charges,” including “computer generated images or deepfakes” and “virtual depictions of minors.” The Wikipedia overview on “Legal status of fictional pornography depicting minors” likewise explains that § 1466A makes it illegal to produce, distribute, or possess visual representations such as drawings or cartoons that appear to depict minors in sexually explicit conduct, and that “it is not a required element of any offense under this section that the minor depicted actually exist.”

DOJ / FBI enforcement involving AI‑generated CSAM

A January 2026 FBI interview from the Pittsburgh field office reported that “cyber tips year over year right now are up over 50%,” and highlighted that in 2023 the Pittsburgh FBI “became the first federal agency in U.S. history to successfully prosecute a case involving AI‑generated child sexual abuse material.” In that case, defendant James Smelko was sentenced to prison after possessing and accessing content that digitally superimposed the faces of child actors onto sexual images and videos—i.e., deepfake CSAM created by combining children’s faces with pornographic bodies.

Legal status of “aged‑up” fictional characters

Federal law turns on whether the image “depicts a minor” or “appears to be” a minor, plus obscenity or lack of serious value; it does not ask whether the character has a canonical age in some other medium. The legal commentary on § 1466A emphasizes that it applies broadly to “drawings or cartoons,” “computer generated images,” and “artistic or fantasy illustrations,” and that “no real child must be identified,” which is why anime, manga, and fantasy images have already led to prosecutions under this section.

As a result, an AI‑generated “aged‑up” version of a well‑known child character is unlikely to be safe simply because the creator asserts the character is now 18; if the figure still appears underage to an average adult, prosecutors can argue that the depiction “appears to be” a minor and charge it under § 1466A or related CSAM statutes. Publicly available analyses continue to describe this as a gray area: there do not yet appear to be published federal appellate decisions squarely addressing AI “aged‑up” characters, but existing case law on obscene anime/cartoons and the statutory focus on appearance, not biography, mean there is significant enforcement risk when the depicted person has obviously youthful features, school settings, or other child‑coded attributes.

NCMEC reporting duties and AI platforms

Under 18 U.S.C. § 2258A (not itself AI‑specific), “electronic service providers” (ESPs) that become aware of apparent CSAM on their systems must report it to NCMEC’s CyberTipline; NCMEC’s own overview explains that the CyberTipline, created in 1998, receives reports of suspected child sexual exploitation “from the public and electronic service providers (ESPs).” Generative‑AI providers that host user content, store images, or transmit outputs functionally act as ESPs and, in practice, report suspected CSAM the same way social networks or cloud providers do—through CyberTipline submissions that include uploaded content and metadata.
