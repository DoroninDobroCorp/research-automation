Texas Statutory Framework on Deepfakes and Artificial Intelligence: A Comprehensive Legal Analysis (2019–2026)
1. Executive Summary
As the digital landscape undergoes a seismic shift driven by the proliferation of generative artificial intelligence (AI), the State of Texas has emerged as a vanguard in the legislative battle to regulate synthetic media. The legal environment in Texas regarding deepfakes—digital manipulations of audio, video, or images that realistically depict events that never occurred—has evolved from a narrow, election-focused prohibition in 2019 to a sprawling, multi-code enforcement regime by early 2026. This transformation reflects a legislative recognition that the harms of AI-generated content are not merely reputational or political, but constitute a fundamental assault on individual dignity, privacy, and public safety.
The 89th Legislative Session, concluding in mid-2025, marked a definitive turning point. Faced with the inadequacy of previous statutes to contain the explosion of nonconsensual intimate imagery (NCII) and the commodification of "nudification" technologies, Texas lawmakers enacted a synchronized package of bills—most notably Senate Bill 441 (SB 441), House Bill 149 (HB 149), House Bill 3133 (HB 3133), and House Bill 581 (HB 581). These statutes collectively shifted the state's strategy from simply penalizing the individual creator of a deepfake to imposing strict liability and operational mandates on the entire technological supply chain, including social media platforms, AI developers, and payment processors.
This report offers an exhaustive analysis of this statutory framework. It begins by tracing the historical evolution of Texas deepfake laws, dissecting the limitations of the initial 2019 and 2023 statutes that necessitated the sweeping 2025 reforms. It then provides a granular examination of the current criminal and civil liability landscapes, differentiating between the legal risks facing individual creators versus institutional actors. A significant portion of the analysis is dedicated to the Texas Responsible AI Governance Act (TRAIGA), which establishes a first-in-the-nation regulatory sandbox for AI innovation while strictly prohibiting high-risk applications.
Furthermore, this report scrutinizes the collision between these aggressive state mandates and federal legal protections, specifically the First Amendment and Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act. Through the lens of the landmark State of Texas v. Jorge Abrego case—the first major prosecution under the evolving regime—we illustrate the practical challenges of enforcement. Finally, the report synthesizes these findings into a detailed compliance outlook for stakeholders operating within the Texas jurisdiction, positing that the state has effectively constructed a "digital fortress" that prioritizes victim protection over platform immunity.

2. Historical Context and Legislative Evolution (2019–2023)
To fully comprehend the draconian nature of the 2026 statutes, one must understand the legislative gaps they were designed to fill. The history of deepfake regulation in Texas is a narrative of law playing catch-up with exponential technological advancement.
2.1 The Genesis: Regulating Political Deception (SB 751)
In 2019, deepfake technology was primarily viewed through the lens of national security and election integrity. The "Deepfake Era" was in its infancy, characterized by choppy, obviously manipulated videos of political figures. Responding to fears that such technology could destabilize the democratic process, the 86th Texas Legislature passed Senate Bill 751, effective September 1, 2019.1
2.1.1 Statutory Mechanics of SB 751
SB 751 amended the Texas Election Code by adding Section 255.004. The statute criminalized the creation and distribution of a "deep fake video" if done with the intent to injure a candidate or influence the result of an election.1

	2.1.2 Limitations and Precedents
While pioneering—Texas was the first state to ban political deepfakes—SB 751 was narrowly tailored. It did not address private harms, such as NCII, nor did it cover audio fakes (e.g., voice cloning of a candidate) or static images. Its "intent to deceive" requirement also set a high evidentiary bar for prosecutors, who had to prove the creator's specific mental state regarding the election outcome, rather than just the deceptive nature of the media itself.4 By 2025, as AI voice cloning became indistinguishable from reality (as seen in the New Hampshire "Biden robocall" incident), the limitations of a video-only ban became glaringly apparent, though SB 751 remains the governing statute for election interference as of 2026.5
2.2 The First Response to NCII: Senate Bill 1361 (2023)
Between 2019 and 2023, the threat vector shifted from political disinformation to sexual exploitation. The democratization of "face-swap" technology allowed individuals with no technical expertise to create hyper-realistic pornographic videos using images of classmates, colleagues, or celebrities. In response, the 88th Legislature passed Senate Bill 1361, effective September 1, 2023.6
2.2.1 Codifying Section 21.165
SB 1361 created Penal Code Section 21.165, titled "Unlawful Production or Distribution of Certain Sexually Explicit Videos." This was the state's first attempt to specifically criminalize NCII deepfakes.
Key Provisions of the 2023 Statute:
* Offense: Knowingly producing or distributing a deep fake video without effective consent.
* Content: The video must appear to depict the person with intimate parts exposed or engaged in sexual conduct.
* Consent: The statute relied on the general definition of "effective consent" in the Penal Code.7
2.2.2 The "Video" Loophole
The critical flaw of SB 1361 was its technological specificity. The statute explicitly defined the offense in relation to a "deep fake video." By late 2023 and throughout 2024, the primary tools for harassment were not video generators, but image generators (e.g., Midjourney, Stable Diffusion) and "nudification" bots on messaging apps like Telegram. These tools produced static images, not videos.
Defense attorneys quickly identified this gap. An individual who generated a folder of 100 deepfake nude photos of a victim could technically argue they had not violated Section 21.165, as no "video" was produced. Furthermore, the statute retained language regarding an "intent to deceive," which allowed creators to argue that if they labeled the content as "AI Art" or "Fantasy," they lacked the requisite criminal intent, regardless of the devastation caused to the victim's dignity.9
This "gap period"—from September 2023 to August 2025—left prosecutors with limited tools, forcing them to rely on ill-fitting statutes like "Online Impersonation" or "Harassment," which carried lower penalties and required different evidentiary elements. This legislative lag set the stage for the aggressive overhaul in the 89th Session.

3. The 2025 Overhaul: A Multi-Layered Enforcement Regime
The 89th Legislative Session (2025) represented a paradigm shift. Moving beyond piecemeal criminalization, Texas enacted a comprehensive suite of laws designed to suffocate the deepfake ecosystem. The new regime, fully effective by January 1, 2026, operates on three levels: expanded criminal liability, systemic civil liability for platforms, and proactive AI governance.
3.1 Criminal Liability: The Expansion of SB 441
Senate Bill 441, effective September 1, 2025, is the cornerstone of the current criminal framework. It fundamentally amended Penal Code Section 21.165 to close the loopholes of its predecessor.11
3.1.1 From "Video" to "Media"
The most significant change was the semantic shift from "video" to "media." Section 21.165 is now titled "Unlawful Production or Distribution of Certain Sexually Explicit Media."
* Definition: "Deep fake media" is defined as a visual depiction created or altered by AI or machine learning that appears to a reasonable person to be indistinguishable from an actual visual depiction.13
* Scope: This definition captures all visual formats: video, static images, GIFs, and potentially even holographic projections. It eliminates the "static image" defense that plagued the 2023 statute.15
3.1.2 The "Reasonable Person" Standard
SB 441 removed the requirement that the creator have an "intent to deceive." Instead, the statute focuses on the nature of the content. If a reasonable person would view the image and believe it depicts the victim, the offense is complete.13
* Implication: A creator can no longer evade liability by placing a small "AI Generated" watermark in the corner of a hyper-realistic nude image. If the overall impression is realistic enough to harm the victim's reputation or dignity, the watermark does not negate the offense. This shifts the focus from the perpetrator's mindset to the impact on the victim and the public.15
3.1.3 The Specificity of Consent
The statute redefined "effective consent" in the context of deepfakes. A general release form is no longer sufficient.
* Requirement: Consent must be an "affirmative, conscious, and voluntary agreement" that includes a general description of the deep fake media.15
* Legal Impact: This prevents the misuse of standard modeling contracts. A photographer cannot use outtakes from a standard fashion shoot to train a pornographic AI model unless the model explicitly signed a waiver agreeing to the creation of "AI-generated sexually explicit content."
3.1.4 Penalties and Enhancements
While the base offense remains a Class A Misdemeanor (up to 1 year in jail, $4,000 fine), the enhancements are severe:
* 3rd Degree Felony: If the victim is a minor (under 18) or if the defendant has a prior conviction.17
* 2nd Degree Felony: For repeat offenders or those promoting large quantities (50+ depictions).18
* Collateral Consequences: Conviction results in automatic suspension of a License to Carry (LTC) for 5 years and potential sex offender registration depending on the specific judicial finding.9
3.2 Civil Liability: Deputizing the Supply Chain (Chapter 98B)
Recognizing that criminal prosecution is often slow and creates no financial restitution for victims, the 2025 legislature radically expanded Chapter 98B of the Civil Practice and Remedies Code. This expansion, driven by SB 441, introduces a strict liability regime for the technological enablers of deepfakes.11
3.2.1 The "Nudification" Application
The statute targets the tools of creation. It defines a "nudification application" as software primarily designed or marketed to create artificial intimate visual material.
* Liability: Owners and operators of such applications are civilly liable for damages if they know or recklessly disregard that the depicted person did not consent.13
* Recklessness: Operating a service that allows users to upload any photo and strip the clothing from it, without any verification mechanism, constitutes "reckless disregard" per se. This provision aims to bankrupt the developers of these apps by exposing them to limitless tort liability.12
3.2.2 Payment Processor Liability
In a groundbreaking move, Texas extended liability to the financial rails. Section 98B.0022 holds payment processors liable if they process payments for the production or disclosure of prohibited material while knowing or recklessly disregarding the lack of consent.11
* Market Impact: This forces entities like Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, and PayPal to implement aggressive content moderation policies. If a payment processor services a "deepfake porn" site, they are now a potential co-defendant in a lawsuit. This "financial de-platforming" is a more effective regulatory tool than criminal law in many instances, as it cuts off the monetization of abuse.12
3.2.3 Confidentiality and Statutes of Limitation
Victims of NCII often hesitate to sue due to fear of public exposure. Chapter 98B addresses this by:
* Mandatory Pseudonymity: Courts must allow plaintiffs to proceed using a confidential identity (e.g., "Jane Doe") in all public filings.11
* Extended Limitations: The statute of limitations is 10 years from the date of discovery or the victim's 18th birthday, whichever is later. This recognizes the "permanent" nature of internet content.11
3.3 Platform Operational Mandates (HB 3133 & HB 581)
Beyond liability, Texas imposed specific affirmative duties on platforms.
3.3.1 Rapid Takedown Protocols (HB 3133)
Effective September 1, 2025, HB 3133 mandates that social media platforms implement a specific reporting mechanism for explicit deepfakes.
* 48-Hour Acknowledgment: Platforms must confirm receipt of a report within 48 hours.
* 72-Hour Removal: While the federal "Take It Down Act" allows for a "reasonable" time, Texas civil law creates a strict liability trigger if content is not removed within 72 hours of a valid request.15
* Duplicate Prevention: Platforms must make "reasonable efforts" (typically interpreted as hash-matching) to prevent the reposting of identical material.20
3.3.2 Age Verification and "Supply Side" Control (HB 581)
HB 581 targets the generators. Commercial entities providing AI generation tools must verify that users are 18 or older.
* Verification: Must use government ID or commercially reasonable transactional data.
* Source Consent: The law requires that the source (the person depicted) must have consented if the output is sexually explicit.
* Penalty: Civil penalties of up to $10,000 per day for operating without verification, escalating to $250,000 if a minor accesses harmful material.21

4. Comprehensive AI Governance: The TRAIGA Framework (HB 149)
While SB 441 and HB 3133 address specific harms (pornography), House Bill 149—the Texas Responsible AI Governance Act (TRAIGA)—establishes a foundational regulatory architecture for AI in Texas. Effective January 1, 2026, TRAIGA positions Texas as a distinct regulatory jurisdiction alongside the EU and Colorado.22
4.1 Prohibited AI Practices: The "Intent" Standard
TRAIGA adopts a risk-based approach but defines "high-risk" through the lens of developer intent. It prohibits the development or deployment of AI systems with the intent or sole intent to:
1. Manipulate Human Behavior: To incite crime, violence, or self-harm.
2. Unlawfully Discriminate: Against protected classes (race, sex, disability, etc.).
3. Produce Illegal Content: Specifically, AI systems intended to produce CSAM or prohibited deepfakes.24
Crucial Distinction: Unlike the EU AI Act, which penalizes outcomes (disparate impact), TRAIGA explicitly states that "disparate impact is not sufficient" to prove a violation.26 This is a business-friendly guardrail: a developer is not liable if their model accidentally exhibits bias, provided they did not design it to discriminate. This "intent-based" standard is unique to Texas and reflects the state's pro-business regulatory philosophy.
4.2 The Regulatory Sandbox
TRAIGA creates an Artificial Intelligence Regulatory Sandbox administered by the Department of Information Resources (DIR).
* Objective: To encourage innovation by allowing companies to test AI systems for a limited period (36 months) without the burden of full regulatory compliance (e.g., licensing requirements).
* Mechanics: Applicants must submit a detailed plan, including risk mitigation strategies and public safety assessments.
* Oversight: The newly established Texas Artificial Intelligence Council monitors the sandbox and advises the legislature on future regulations.23
* Limitation: The sandbox does not provide immunity from criminal laws or the specific deepfake prohibitions of SB 441. It is designed for administrative and licensure relief, not criminal immunity.28
4.3 Enforcement
Enforcement of TRAIGA is centralized in the Office of the Attorney General. There is no private right of action for citizens under HB 149.
* Cure Period: Entities generally have a 60-day window to cure non-compliance after being notified by the AG.
* Penalties: Civil penalties range from $10,000 to $200,000 per violation, depending on whether the violation is curable.26

5. Federal Preemption and Constitutional Friction
The aggressiveness of the Texas statutes creates inevitable friction with federal law. As of 2026, the legal landscape is defined by the tension between state police powers and federal protections for speech and internet commerce.
5.1 The First Amendment: Parody and "Reasonable Persons"
Deepfakes occupy a precarious position in First Amendment jurisprudence. While obscenity (NCII) is generally unprotected, political deepfakes and satire are core protected speech.
* The "Reasonable Person" Test: SB 441 relies on whether a "reasonable person" would find the media indistinguishable from reality. Critics argue this is vague. Does a hyper-realistic satire of a politician "appear to be" the politician? If so, it might violate the statute unless a court reads a parody exception into the "reasonable person" analysis.
* HB 783 (Catfishing): This statute explicitly includes a parody exception.29 The absence of such an exception in SB 441 (the porn statute) suggests the legislature intended to prioritize victim privacy over satirical expression in the context of sexual imagery.30
5.2 Section 230 and the DTPA Workaround
Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (CDA) immunizes interactive computer services from liability for third-party content. Texas's HB 3133 (Takedowns) and Chapter 98B (Facilitation Liability) appear to conflict directly with this federal shield.
The "Deceptive Trade Practice" Strategy: Texas attempts to circumvent Section 230 by framing the violation not as publishing content, but as deceptive business conduct. The argument posits that platforms violate the Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA) when they fail to adhere to their own safety policies or the state's mandated takedown procedures.31 By regulating the "conduct" of the business (failure to remove) rather than the "speech" itself, Texas hopes to survive a preemption challenge.
* NetChoice v. Paxton: The Supreme Court's 2024 ruling on social media moderation laws left the door slightly ajar for state regulation of platform conduct that does not infringe on editorial discretion. Texas is exploiting this opening, arguing that removing illegal NCII is a safety compliance issue, not an editorial one.32
5.3 Federal Overlap: The "Take It Down Act"
The federal Take It Down Act, signed in May 2025, also mandates notice-and-takedown procedures. However, the federal act gives platforms a "reasonable" time to remove content, whereas Texas HB 3133 mandates a strict 72-hour removal for civil liability purposes.15 Platforms operating in Texas must default to the stricter state standard to avoid liability under Chapter 98B.

6. Case Study: State of Texas v. Jorge Abrego
The theoretical complexities of these laws were brought into sharp focus in late 2025 with the prosecution of Jorge Abrego. This case serves as a bellwether for enforcement trends and statutory application.
6.1 Factual Background
Jorge Abrego, co-owner of "HTX Tactical," a Houston-based business, was arrested in August 2025. Investigators alleged that Abrego used AI tools to generate over 50 deepfake nude images and videos of a local social media influencer ("Jane Doe"). He allegedly created fake TikTok accounts to distribute this material while simultaneously befriending the victim in person.34
6.2 The Charging Dilemma
Abrego was arrested in August 2025, just weeks before the effective date of the new SB 441 (Sept 1, 2025). This timing created a significant legal hurdle.
* Applicable Law: Prosecutors were forced to charge him under the 2023 version of Penal Code 21.165 (SB 1361).
* The "Video" Requirement: The 2023 law only criminalized deepfake videos. If Abrego's output consisted primarily of static images, the defense could move to dismiss those counts. Fortunately for prosecutors, evidence suggested he produced both videos and images, allowing the charges to stick.36
* Intent to Deceive: Prosecutors also had to prove "intent to deceive." The creation of fake TikTok accounts impersonating the victim provided the necessary evidence of deceptive intent, which might have been harder to prove if he had simply posted them anonymously on a forum.35
6.3 Civil Litigation Strategy
In January 2026, the Buzbee Law Firm filed a $10 million civil lawsuit against Abrego and his business, HTX Tactical.
* Vicarious Liability: The inclusion of HTX Tactical as a defendant is legally significant. It suggests the plaintiffs are arguing that business resources (internet, computers, or time) were used to facilitate the abuse. If successful, this would establish a precedent that employers can be liable for AI-based harassment committed by employees using company assets.35
* Causes of Action: The suit likely leverages the new Chapter 98C (Online Impersonation) for the fake accounts and common law torts for the deepfakes, as the conduct spanned into the effective period of the new civil statutes.

7. Compliance Guidelines for Stakeholders (2026)
The Texas framework imposes a high compliance burden. Stakeholders must adopt a "Texas-specific" or "highest-common-denominator" approach to operations.
7.1 Compliance for Social Media Platforms
* Dedicated Reporting Portal: Must maintain a user-friendly system specifically for reporting "explicit deep fake material" (HB 3133).
* SLA Enforcement: Strict adherence to the 48-hour acknowledgment and 72-hour removal timelines is non-negotiable to avoid civil liability.
* Hash Matching: Implementation of automated technologies to detect and block re-uploads of known deepfakes is required to meet the "reasonable efforts" standard.20
7.2 Compliance for AI Developers and "Nudification" Tools
* Age Gating: Any tool capable of generating realistic images must implement robust age verification (government ID or transactional data) to ensure users are 18+ (HB 581).
* Intent Documentation: Developers must document their safety testing ("red-teaming") to prove they did not intend for their system to be used for illegal content generation, providing a defense against TRAIGA liability.26
* Terms of Service: Must explicitly prohibit the generation of NCII and nonconsensual content.
7.3 Compliance for Payment Processors
* Merchant Audit: Financial institutions must audit merchants to identify high-risk "nudification" applications.
* KYC/KYB: Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) is required for any merchant in the generative AI space to ensure they have consent mechanisms in place. Processing payments for a non-compliant app creates direct liability under Chapter 98B.11

8. Conclusion
As of February 2026, Texas has constructed the most formidable anti-deepfake legal infrastructure in the United States. By moving beyond the simple criminalization of the individual actor, the state has targeted the economic and technical substrates that allow deepfakes to flourish. The Abrego case demonstrates the state's willingness to enforce these laws criminally, while the new civil statutes incentivize a wave of private litigation against platforms and enablers.
For the technology sector, the message from Austin is clear: the era of "permissionless innovation" regarding synthetic media is over. The "Texas Model"—characterized by strict liability, rapid takedowns, and the deputization of financial intermediaries—may well become the template for future federal regulation or adoption by other states. Stakeholders must now operate under the assumption that in Texas, the right to digital privacy and dignity supersedes the immunity traditionally afforded to the digital ecosystem.
