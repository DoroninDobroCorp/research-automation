State of the Union: A Comprehensive Analysis of Deepfake and AI-Generated Content Legislation Across the United States (January 2026)
Executive Summary
As of January 2026, the legislative landscape regarding artificial intelligence (AI), deepfakes, and synthetic media in the United States has matured from a nascent collection of scattered privacy laws into a complex, multi-jurisdictional regulatory patchwork. The proliferation of generative AI technologies—capable of creating hyper-realistic digital replicas, voice clones, and non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII)—has forced state legislatures to act with unprecedented speed. While the federal government enacted the Take It Down Act in May 2025 to establish a baseline for removing non-consensual sexual content 1, the substantive governance of AI, particularly regarding property rights, election integrity, and commercial liability, remains the domain of state law.
This report provides an exhaustive analysis of the statutory frameworks across all 50 states. As of early 2026, 47 states have enacted some form of legislation specifically targeting deepfakes or AI-generated content.2 These laws vary dramatically in scope, ranging from narrow criminal enhancements for "revenge porn" to comprehensive "Digital Bill of Rights" frameworks that establish new property interests in one’s voice and likeness.
The analysis classifies states into three distinct tiers based on the rigor and breadth of their regulatory regimes:
1. Strict/Comprehensive Frameworks: States that have enacted omnibus AI governance, created novel intellectual property rights, or established strict liability regimes for platforms and developers (e.g., Tennessee, California, Illinois, Colorado, New York, Texas).
2. Moderate/Sector-Specific Frameworks: States with targeted statutes addressing specific harms—primarily election interference and sexual privacy—without broader AI governance structures (e.g., Pennsylvania, Michigan, Arizona, Florida).
3. Limited/No Specific Frameworks: States where AI-specific legislation has either stalled or has not yet been introduced, forcing reliance on general criminal statutes (e.g., Alaska, Missouri, Ohio).
Crucially, January 2026 marks a period of significant transition. Major statutes in Texas (the Responsible AI Governance Act) and California (regarding companion chatbots) have just entered into force, and the interaction between these state laws and the new federal preemption standards remains a critical area of legal friction.

Part I: States with Strict and Comprehensive Regulatory Frameworks
This category encompasses states that have moved beyond reactive prohibitions to establish proactive governance models. These jurisdictions have fundamentally redefined legal concepts of privacy, property, and liability to accommodate the reality of synthetic media.
1.1 Tennessee: The Global Standard for Right of Publicity
Driven by its identity as a global music capital, Tennessee has enacted the most aggressive protections for voice and likeness in the nation, setting a precedent that other states are now attempting to emulate.
Statute: The ELVIS Act (Ensuring Likeness, Voice, and Image Security)
Citation: SB 1188 / HB 2091 (Enacted 2024; Active 2026) 3
Scope and Coverage: The ELVIS Act is a landmark statute that explicitly modernizes the state's right of publicity to include "voice" as a protectable property right.5 Unlike traditional publicity laws that focus on name and image, the ELVIS Act targets the specific capability of generative AI to clone vocal characteristics. It covers commercial uses, but its definition of "commercial" is broad enough to encompass unauthorized digital replicas used in entertainment and media.
Criminal and Civil Penalties:
Tennessee has adopted a dual-enforcement mechanism:
* Criminal Liability: Violation of the ELVIS Act is a Class A Misdemeanor, punishable by up to 11 months and 29 days in jail and fines up to $2,500.6
* Civil Liability: The statute allows for civil actions seeking injunctions, actual damages, and disgorgement of profits. Crucially, in cases of "knowing" infringement, the court may award treble damages (triple the actual damages), significantly raising the financial stakes for unauthorized cloning.7
Platform Liability Provisions: The ELVIS Act is notable for expanding liability beyond the creator of the deepfake to the technology providers. It targets entities that "make available" the software or tools used to create unauthorized replicas if they have knowledge that the primary purpose of the tool is infringement.8 This provision attempts to pierce the traditional immunity provided by Section 230 of the federal Communications Decency Act by framing the liability around intellectual property violations (which are often exempt from Section 230).
Consent and Disclosure: The law mandates explicit, written consent for the use of an individual's voice or likeness. For deceased individuals, this right is descendible and persists for at least 10 years (and potentially longer if the likeness remains in commercial use), requiring consent from the estate.8
1.2 California: The Regulatory Battleground
California, as the home of Silicon Valley and the entertainment industry, has enacted the highest volume of AI legislation. Its framework is characterized by a "stack" of specific laws targeting different aspects of the AI ecosystem, from chatbots to employment contracts.
Statute A: The Companion Chatbot Law
Citation: SB 243 (Effective January 1, 2026) 9 Scope: This statute regulates "companion chatbots"—AI systems designed to simulate human relationships. Disclosure/Consent: Operators must clearly disclose that the bot is an AI. Platform Liability: The law imposes strict safety protocols. If the operator knows the user is a minor, they must:
* Provide safety notifications every three hours ("take a break" warnings).
* Implement filters to prevent the generation of sexually explicit content (CSAM) or content encouraging self-harm.10 Penalties: The law provides for civil actions for "injury in fact," allowing users (or parents) to sue for damages resulting from the bot's failure to adhere to safety protocols.10
Statute B: Digital Replicas in Employment
Citation: AB 2602 (Effective January 1, 2025) 3 Scope: Commercial/Employment. Provisions: This law protects performers from having their digital replicas used to replace them. It renders unenforceable any contract provision that allows for the creation/use of a digital replica of a voice or likeness in lieu of actual work, unless the contract includes a reasonably specific description of the intended use and the performer was represented by legal counsel or a labor union.4
Statute C: The AI Transparency Act
Citation: SB 942 (Effective August 2026; provisions active Jan 2026) 12 Scope: Commercial/Consumer Protection. Requirements: Requires large generative AI providers (those with >1 million monthly visitors) to provide a free AI detection tool and to include latent watermarks or metadata in their outputs to verify provenance.3
Note on Election Laws: California's aggressive attempt to ban "materially deceptive" election deepfakes (AB 2839 and AB 2655) was enjoined by a federal court in late 2024 and remains blocked as of January 2026 on First Amendment grounds.13
1.3 Illinois: Biometric and Employment Protections
Illinois continues its tradition of strict privacy regulation (stemming from BIPA) by expanding protections into the AI workplace.
Statute: Human Rights Act Amendment (AI in Employment)
Citation: HB 3773 (Effective January 1, 2026) 15 Scope: Employment/Commercial. Coverage: The law amends the Illinois Human Rights Act to explicitly prohibit the use of AI in recruitment, hiring, promotion, or discharge if it has the effect of subjecting employees to discrimination based on protected classes (race, zip code, etc.).16 Consent/Disclosure: Employers must notify employees when AI is being used in hiring decisions.17 Penalties: Violations are treated as civil rights offenses, subject to the remedies available under the Human Rights Act, which can include uncapped compensatory damages and attorney's fees.
1.4 New York: Advertising and Post-Mortem Rights
New York's 2025-2026 session focused heavily on the advertising sector, recognizing the threat of "synthetic performers" to the human workforce.
Statute A: Synthetic Performers in Advertising
Citation: S. 8420-A (Enacted Dec 2025; Effective 2026) 18 Scope: Commercial/Advertising. Requirements: Requires "clear and conspicuous" disclosure in any advertisement that utilizes a "synthetic performer" (a digital asset intended to act as a human). Penalties: Civil penalties of $1,000 for a first violation and $5,000 for subsequent violations.18
Statute B: Digital Replica Rights
Citation: S. 8391 (Active) 20 Scope: Right of Publicity. Provisions: Expands the right of publicity for deceased personalities to prevent the unauthorized use of digital replicas. This closes a loophole where AI could be used to resurrect deceased actors for commercial gain without estate permission.21
1.5 Texas: The Governance Model
Texas has adopted an omnibus governance approach, distinct from the liability-heavy models of California and Tennessee.
Statute: Texas Responsible AI Governance Act (TRAIGA)
Citation: HB 2091 (Effective January 1, 2026) 22 Scope: Omnibus/Governance. Provisions: TRAIGA establishes a framework for the development and deployment of "high-risk" AI systems. It explicitly prohibits the use of AI for social scoring or biometric capture without consent. Deepfakes: It reinforces existing Election Code § 255.004, which criminalizes the creation of deepfake videos within 30 days of an election intended to injure a candidate.24 Penalties: Enforcement is vested exclusively in the Attorney General (no private right of action), with civil penalties up to $100,000 for violations.22
1.6 Colorado: The Risk-Based Framework
Citation: SB 24-205 (Colorado AI Act) 12 Effective Date: February 1, 2026. Scope: Focuses on "High-Risk AI Systems" in consequential decision-making areas like housing, employment, and finance. Deepfake Provisions: HB 24-1147 specifically mandates clear disclosures for candidate election deepfakes. Failure to disclose allows for civil penalties and injunctive relief sought by the candidate.25

Part III: States with Limited or No Specific Frameworks
As of January 2026, a diminishing number of states have not enacted specific statutes targeting deepfakes. In these jurisdictions, prosecutors and plaintiffs must rely on general criminal statutes (harassment, fraud, menacing) or federal law.
3.1 States with Active but Unenacted Bills (2026 Session)
* Alaska:
   * Status: Limited/None Enacted.
   * Activity: SB 2 was introduced in January 2026 to address deepfake disclosures, following the failure of SB 64 in the previous session.49 Currently, Alaska relies on general privacy laws.
* Missouri:
   * Status: Limited.
   * Activity: HB 2321 (AI Accountability) and HB 2862 (Deepfake Impersonation) were introduced in 2026.50 No specific statute is currently in force.
* Iowa:
   * Status: Limited.
   * Activity: HF 2239 was introduced in Jan 2026.51 Iowa does not have a comprehensive deepfake statute enacted as of Jan 2026.
* Ohio:
   * Status: Limited.
   * Activity: Ohio is listed as one of the few states with no specific deepfake legislation enacted as of 2026.52 Legislative efforts have stalled, forcing reliance on federal NCII laws.
3.2 The Legal Gap and Federal Reliance
In these "Limited" states, the legal recourse for victims is significantly narrower:
1. NCII: Victims must rely on the federal Take It Down Act (May 2025) to force removal of content from platforms. State-level prosecution is difficult unless the conduct meets the strict elements of traditional harassment or obscenity.
2. Commercial Cloning: Unlike Tennessee or California, citizens in these states have no explicit "digital voice right." A musician in Ohio whose voice is cloned has limited recourse under standard Right of Publicity laws, which may not explicitly cover "synthetic" voice without a visual likeness.
3. Election Deepfakes: Without specific "90-day" prohibitions (like Michigan or Arizona), misleading political deepfakes are arguably protected speech under the First Amendment unless they constitute defamation, which has a very high burden of proof for public figures.

Part IV: Comparative Analysis of Key Legal Mechanisms
4.1 Platform Liability: The Section 230 Frontier
State legislatures are increasingly aggressive in attempting to bypass the immunity provided to platforms by Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act.
* The "Tool Provider" Loophole: Tennessee’s ELVIS Act sidesteps Section 230 by targeting the provision of tools rather than the hosting of content. By framing the AI software itself as a tool for intellectual property theft, Tennessee creates a liability risk for generative AI companies that Section 230 (which protects publishers of third-party content) may not cover.
* Knowledge Standards: States like Arizona and Pennsylvania include "knowledge" requirements. If a platform is notified (has actual knowledge) that content is a malicious deepfake and fails to act, states argue they become content providers/conspirators.
* Federal Floor: The Take It Down Act creates a standardized federal notice-and-takedown system. Platforms operating in all 50 states will likely default to this federal standard to minimize compliance costs, effectively raising the baseline even in states with no laws.
4.2 Consent and Disclosure Standards
* Implicit vs. Explicit Consent:
   * Strict States (TN, CA, NY): Require written, informed consent specifically for digital replication. A general release form is insufficient; the contract must specify the use of AI.
   * Moderate States: Often assume lack of consent if the context is harmful (e.g., NCII), without requiring specific contractual formalities for commercial use.
* Disclosure Formats:
   * Human-Readable: Most election laws (MI, WI, FL) require on-screen text or audio announcements ("This content generated by AI").
   * Machine-Readable: California’s SB 942 and Colorado’s AI Act push for "watermarking" and metadata (C2PA standards) that allow platforms to automatically label content. This represents the future of compliance—technological rather than purely legal.
4.3 Penalties: The Shift to Felony Classification
There is a clear trend in 2026 toward upgrading penalties:
* NCII/CSAM: Formerly treated as misdemeanors in "revenge porn" statutes, AI-generated sexual abuse materials are now consistently classified as Felonies (e.g., Virginia Class 6 Felony, Pennsylvania 3rd Degree Felony).
* Election Interference: Generally remains a Misdemeanor (to avoid chilling speech too aggressively), but with high civil penalties (injunctive relief, attorney fees) that serve as a functional deterrent for campaigns.

Part V: Future Outlook (2026-2027)
5.1 The "Digital Replica" Wave
Following the success of Tennessee's ELVIS Act, 2026 will see a proliferation of "Digital Replica" bills in other states with creative economies (Georgia, Louisiana). The concept of a "post-mortem digital right" will likely become standard, preventing the unauthorized "resurrection" of deceased public figures.20
5.2 Constitutional Litigation
The injunctions against California's election deepfake laws (AB 2839/2655) highlight the fragility of these regulations. In 2026-2027, the Supreme Court may eventually have to clarify the line between prohibited fraud and protected satire in the context of AI. States with broad bans (no safe harbor for satire) are vulnerable to legal challenges.
5.3 Federal Preemption Battles
The Trump Administration’s Executive Order (Dec 2025) aiming for a "minimally burdensome" national AI policy 53 sets the stage for conflict. If the Department of Justice views strict state laws (like Colorado's AI Act) as impediments to U.S. AI competitiveness, we may see federal preemption lawsuits attempting to void state-level AI safety requirements.
________________
Conclusion
As of January 2026, the United States regulatory environment for AI is defined by a sharp divide. A "Strict Bloc" of states (led by Tennessee, California, and Illinois) has erected a fortress of property rights and transparency mandates that impose significant duties on AI developers. A "Moderate Bloc" has effectively criminalized the most egregious misuses (sexual privacy and election fraud) without impeding the technology's broader development. Finally, a shrinking "Limited Bloc" remains vulnerable to the harms of synthetic media, relying on federal backstops.
For platforms and creators, the compliance reality is determined by the strictest common denominator: the rigorous disclosure and property rights regimes of California and Tennessee now effectively set the national standard for any entity operating at scale.
