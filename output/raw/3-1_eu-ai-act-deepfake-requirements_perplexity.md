Under the final EU AI Act text, the deepfake and synthetic-content transparency rules are now in Article 50 (earlier drafts called this Article 52), and they impose disclosure duties both on providers (marking outputs in a machine‑readable way) and on deployers (visible labeling to users) when content is AI‑generated or manipulated.

Where the deepfake rule sits (Article 50 vs 52)

In the compromise and final text, the transparency chapter was renumbered so that what was Article 52 in older drafts is now Article 50 “Transparency obligations for providers and deployers of certain AI systems and of general‑purpose AI models.” The specific deepfake rule appears in paragraph 4 of Article 50 and is the operative provision for AI‑generated or AI‑manipulated image, audio and video content.

Exact deepfake requirements in Article 50(4)

Article 50(4) defines “deep fake” obligations for deployers of AI systems that generate or manipulate image, audio or video content constituting a deepfake: they must disclose that the content has been artificially generated or manipulated. A deepfake is understood (following earlier analyses of the same provision) as content that appreciably resembles existing persons, places or events and would falsely appear to a user as authentic or truthful.

The obligation does not apply when the AI system is used under legal authorisation to detect, prevent, investigate or prosecute criminal offences. Where the content forms part of an “evidently artistic, creative, satirical, fictional or analogous work or programme,” the rule is limited to disclosure of the existence of such generated or manipulated content, in a way that does not hamper display or enjoyment of the work.

In addition, the same paragraph extends disclosure duties to text generated or manipulated by AI where it is “published with the purpose of informing the public on matters of public interest.” For this text‑disclosure duty there are two exemptions: (a) use authorised for law‑enforcement purposes, and (b) where the AI‑generated content has undergone human review/editorial control and a natural or legal person holds editorial responsibility for the publication.

Provider vs deployer duties and labeling standards

Beyond paragraph 4, Article 50 also imposes general transparency rules on providers of AI and GPAI models: they must ensure that synthetic outputs are marked in a machine‑readable format and are detectable as artificially generated or manipulated. This is intended to support downstream labeling by platforms and other deployers, for example through watermarks or other technical signals.

Deployers (users, such as platforms) of systems that produce realistic synthetic content must then clearly inform natural persons that they are seeing AI‑generated or AI‑manipulated content, at the latest at the moment of first exposure. Commission‑linked work on a Code of Practice for deepfake labeling suggests use of a common icon plus context‑appropriate on‑screen, audio or textual disclaimers, provided “clearly, distinguishably and accessibly.”

Compliance timeline 2025–2027

The AI Act entered into force in 2024 with a general 24‑month transition period, meaning most “main regime” obligations—such as Article 50 transparency rules—apply by August 2, 2026 unless a specific earlier or later date is prescribed.
