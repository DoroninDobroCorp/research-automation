# California’s AI, Deepfake, and Digital Likeness Laws (2024–2026): Comprehensive Research Report

## Executive Summary
As of February 17, 2026, California has established one of the world's most rigorous legal frameworks for artificial intelligence, digital likeness, and deepfakes. The state's strategy has bifurcated into two distinct tracks: **enforceable strict liability** for commercial and sexually exploitative uses of AI, and **constitutionally stalled** attempts to regulate political speech.

For businesses and platforms, the landscape has shifted from voluntary safety guidelines to mandatory compliance. **Civil Code § 3344** now explicitly covers "digital replicas" and empowers courts to issue 48-hour takedown orders for unauthorized AI clones of living persons [1]. Similarly, **AB 1836** has closed the post-mortem loophole, granting estates control over digital resurrections of deceased personalities for 70 years [2].

The most aggressive enforcement is currently targeted at nonconsensual sexual deepfakes. With the enactment of **SB 926** (criminal liability) and **SB 981** (platform takedown mandates), coupled with the Attorney General's January 2026 cease-and-desist order against xAI's "Grok," the state has signaled zero tolerance for AI-generated exploitation [3] [4]. Conversely, California's ambitious election deepfake laws (**AB 2839** and **AB 2655**) were permanently enjoined by federal courts in late 2025 on First Amendment and Section 230 grounds, leaving voluntary labeling and the narrower **AB 2355** disclosure as the primary election safeguards [5] [6].

### Top Strategic Decisions for 2026
* **Talent Contracting:** All agreements involving AI voice/likeness must now include "reasonably specific descriptions" of use and proof of counsel/union representation, or they are void under **Labor Code § 927** [7].
* **Platform Operations:** Social platforms must maintain a dedicated reporting queue for sexual deepfakes with strict statutory timelines (48-hour acknowledgment, temporary blocking) to avoid UCL liability [8].
* **AI Development:** Generative AI providers must prepare for the **August 2, 2026** operative date of **SB 942**, which mandates free detection tools and latent watermarking for AI outputs [9] [10].

## Right of Publicity: Living Persons (Civil Code § 3344)

California's primary right of publicity statute has been modernized to address the speed and scale of generative AI.

### Digital Replicas and Injunctive Relief
**SB 683**, effective January 1, 2026, explicitly amended Civil Code § 3344 to include "digital replicas" within the definition of a protected voice or likeness [1]. Crucially, it introduced a rapid response mechanism for victims of unauthorized AI cloning:
* **Injunctive Relief:** Plaintiffs may seek a Temporary Restraining Order (TRO) under Code of Civil Procedure § 527.
* **48-Hour Compliance:** If a court orders the removal or cessation of a digital replica, the respondent must comply within **two business days** of service [1].

### Liability and Damages
Violators who knowingly use a digital replica for advertising or sales without consent face:
* **Statutory Damages:** The greater of **$750** or actual damages [20].
* **Profits:** Any profits attributable to the unauthorized use (plaintiff proves gross revenue; defendant must prove deductible expenses) [20].
* **Punitive Damages:** Available for willful violations [20].
* **Attorney's Fees:** Mandatory for the prevailing party [20].

### Exemptions
Section 3344(d) retains exemptions for uses in connection with **news, public affairs, sports broadcasts, or political campaigns** [20]. However, courts have increasingly narrowed these exemptions when the use is commercial or lacks transformative value (see Case Law below).

## Right of Publicity: Deceased Personalities (Civil Code § 3344.1)

**AB 1836**, effective January 1, 2025, closed a significant gap regarding the "reanimation" of deceased celebrities.

### Scope and Prohibitions
The law prohibits producing, distributing, or making available a **digital replica** of a deceased personality's voice or likeness in an **expressive audiovisual work or sound recording** without prior consent from the rights holder [2].
* **Term:** Protection lasts for **70 years** after death [17].
* **Retroactivity:** Applies to all deceased personalities who died on or after January 1, 1915 (retroactive to those who died before 1985) [21].

### Damages and Standing
* **Statutory Minimum:** Liability is the greater of **$10,000** or actual damages [2].
* **Registration:** Successors in interest must register their claim with the California Secretary of State to have standing to sue [17].
* **Music Industry:** Specific standing is granted to record labels or entities with exclusive contracts for the individual's audio performances [22].

### Exemptions
The law exempts uses that are:
* News, public affairs, or sports broadcasts.
* Comment, criticism, scholarship, satire, or parody.
* Documentary/biographical representations (unless they create a false impression of an authentic recording) [17].
* Fleeting or incidental uses [17].

## Sexual Deepfakes and Platform Duties

This area represents the most active enforcement front, combining civil liability, criminal penalties, and platform mandates.

### Civil Liability (Civil Code § 1708.86)
Amended by **AB 621** (effective Jan 1, 2026), this statute targets "deepfake pornography services" (e.g., nudification apps) and their facilitators.
* **Facilitator Liability:** Service providers (hosting, payments) are presumed liable if they fail to stop servicing a violator within **30 days** of receiving evidence [15].
* **Damages:** Up to **$150,000** in statutory damages if committed with malice (increased to $250,000 under AB 621 amendments) [23].
* **Public Enforcement:** Public prosecutors can seek civil penalties of **$25,000** per violation ($50,000 for malice) [15].

### Criminal Liability (Penal Code § 647(j)(4))
**SB 926** (effective Jan 1, 2025) makes it a misdemeanor to intentionally create and distribute sexually explicit deepfakes if the creator knows or should know it will cause serious emotional distress [12].
* **Scope:** Applies to photorealistic images of intimate body parts or sexual acts.
* **Penalty:** Up to 6 months in jail and/or $1,000 fine; repeat offenses can be felonies [24].

### Platform Obligations (SB 981)
Effective January 1, 2025, social media platforms must maintain a reporting mechanism for "sexually explicit digital identity theft" [8].
* **48 Hours:** Deadline to confirm receipt of a report.
* **Temporary Block:** Content must be blocked from public view pending investigation.
* **14-30 Days:** Deadline to make a final determination (extendable to 60 days for cause) [8].
* **Permanent Removal:** Required if the content is verified as a nonconsensual deepfake.

## Voice Cloning & Contracting (AB 2602)

**Labor Code § 927**, enacted by AB 2602, regulates the use of AI to replace human performers.

### Enforceability Requirements
A contract provision allowing the use of a digital replica in place of a human performance is **unenforceable** unless:
1. **Specific Description:** The contract includes a "reasonably specific description" of the intended uses of the digital replica [7].
2. **Representation:** The individual was represented by either:
 * **Legal Counsel:** Who negotiated the terms, with commercial terms stated "clearly and conspicuously" in a signed writing.
 * **Labor Union:** Under a collective bargaining agreement (e.g., SAG-AFTRA) that expressly addresses digital replicas [7].

### Impact
Broad, perpetual grants of rights (e.g., "in any and all media, throughout the universe") are now void for digital replicas in California contracts fixed on or after January 1, 2025 [7].

## Election Deepfakes: Constitutional Limits

While California attempted to ban deceptive election deepfakes, federal courts have blocked these efforts, leaving disclosure as the primary tool.

### Enjoined Statutes
* **AB 2839:** Sought to ban distribution of materially deceptive election media. **Permanently enjoined** on August 29, 2025, in *Kohls v. Bonta* for violating the First Amendment (overbreadth and viewpoint discrimination) [5].
* **AB 2655:** Required platforms to remove/label deceptive content. **Permanently enjoined** on August 20, 2025, as preempted by Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act [5].

### Active Requirements (AB 2355)
* **Disclosure:** Political committees must include the disclosure "Ad generated or substantially altered using artificial intelligence" on qualified ads [25].
* **Enforcement:** The Fair Political Practices Commission (FPPC) enforces this with administrative penalties up to **$5,000 per violation** [25].

## CCPA/CPRA Implications for AI Data

California privacy law treats AI training data and biometric inputs as regulated personal information.

### Key Classifications
* **Personal Information (PI):** **AB 1008** (2024) clarified that PI can exist in "abstract digital formats," including AI systems capable of outputting personal data [26].
* **Sensitive Personal Information (SPI):** Includes **biometric information** (face/voiceprints) processed for identification and **neural data** (added by SB 1223) [26].

### Compliance Obligations
* **Right to Limit:** Consumers can limit the use of SPI (including voice/face data) to what is necessary for the immediate service [27].
* **Automated Decisionmaking (ADMT):** CPPA regulations adopted July 2025 require businesses to provide **Pre-use Notices** and **Opt-Out** rights for ADMT used in significant decisions (e.g., hiring, lending) starting in 2026/2027 [28].
* **Data Minimization:** Collection and retention of training data must be "reasonably necessary and proportionate" [29].

## How Platforms Can Comply

To navigate this complex regime, platforms and AI providers should implement the following controls:

### 1. Provenance and Watermarking (SB 942)
* **Requirement:** By August 2, 2026, GenAI providers (>1M monthly users) must embed **latent disclosures** (invisible watermarks) in image/video/audio outputs containing provider name, system version, and timestamp [30].
* **Action:** Implement **C2PA** standards (Content Credentials) for cryptographic binding and robust watermarking. Provide a free, public API for content verification [30].

### 2. Sexual Deepfake Reporting (SB 981)
* **Requirement:** Maintain a reporting channel for nonconsensual intimate content.
* **SLA Protocol:**
 * **48 Hours:** Send automated confirmation of receipt.
 * **Immediate:** Temporarily block content from public view.
 * **7 Days:** Send status update to reporter.
 * **14-30 Days:** Final determination and permanent removal if verified [8].

### 3. Data Rights Management (CCPA/AB 2602)
* **Consent Management:** Update intake flows to capture "reasonably specific" consent for voice/likeness cloning. Ensure separate, clear sign-offs for digital replica rights [7].
* **Opt-Outs:** Implement "Limit Use of My Sensitive Personal Information" links for biometric data used in AI training [27].

### 4. Impersonation Detection
* **Monitoring:** Deploy detection tools to identify AI-generated impersonation of real persons, which violates Penal Code § 528.5 (e-personation) and § 530.5 (identity theft) [31].
* **Enforcement:** The Attorney General has actively issued C&D orders (e.g., against xAI) for failing to curb nonconsensual deepfake generation [3].
