The Algorithmic Author: Section 230, Generative AI, and the Legal Crisis of Synthetic Adult Content (2024–2026)
Executive Summary

The period between 2024 and early 2026 represents the most significant upheaval in internet jurisprudence since the enactment of the Communications Decency Act (CDA) in 1996. For thirty years, Section 230 of the CDA served as the foundational immunity shield for the internet economy, protecting "interactive computer services" from liability for content created by third parties. This regime, predicated on the distinction between a passive host and an active publisher, has been fundamentally destabilized by the advent of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI).

This report provides an exhaustive analysis of the application of Section 230 to AI-generated adult content—specifically Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery (NCII) and Child Sexual Abuse Material (CSAM)—during this critical two-year window. It argues that the proliferation of "nudify" applications, deepfake generators, and Large Language Models (LLMs) capable of producing illicit imagery has forced a judicial and legislative reckoning. The "intermediary" logic of the 1990s is collapsing under the weight of "algorithmic volition," where software does not merely transmit user speech but actively constructs it.

The analysis details the transition from the "Neutral Tool" doctrine to the "Material Contribution" test, examining how courts in 2025 and 2026 have begun to classify AI models as "Information Content Providers" (ICPs), thereby stripping them of Section 230 immunity. It provides in-depth scrutiny of landmark litigation, including Vance v. Google (2024), Ryan v. X Corp. (2024), and Jane Doe v. ClothOff (2025), and analyzes the transformative impact of the TAKE IT DOWN Act of 2025, which codified a federal exception to Section 230 for non-compliant platforms.

Part I: The Doctrinal Crisis of Section 230 in the Age of AI
1.1 The Statutory Foundation and the "Categorical Error"

To understand the crisis of 2024–2026, one must first analyze the statutory architecture that governed the pre-AI internet. Section 230(c)(1) of the Communications Decency Act provides that "No provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider."  This "twenty-six words" created a bifurcated legal reality: the "Interactive Computer Service" (ICS), which provides the infrastructure, and the "Information Content Provider" (ICP), which provides the content.   

For decades, courts interpreted this broadly. In Zeran v. America Online (1997), the Fourth Circuit ruled that holding a service provider liable for its exercise of traditional editorial functions—such as deciding whether to publish, withdraw, postpone, or alter content—would inherently treat it as a publisher, violating the statute.  This created a regime where platforms were incentivized to moderate voluntarily (the "Good Samaritan" provision of Section 230(c)(2)) but were not penalized for failing to do so.   

However, the integration of Generative AI into online platforms has introduced a "categorical error" in applying these protections. Legal scholars and recent filings argue that GenAI systems defy the ICS/ICP dichotomy. An AI model does not merely "host" information provided by a user; it "hallucinates," synthesizes, and renders pixels that did not previously exist.    

When a user prompts an AI to "create a nude image of [Person X]," the user provides the intent, but the AI provides the expression. The resulting image is a composite of the model's training data (latent space) and its algorithmic weights. Because the "creation" of information is the defining characteristic of an Information Content Provider under Section 230(f)(3), the AI system itself—and by extension, its developer—becomes the ICP.  As argued in the Brown Political Review (2026), treating LLMs as passive intermediaries ignores the technical reality that their outputs are products of internal datasets and learning algorithms. Any harmful statement or image is a result of the system's training and design choices, not merely the user's prompt.    

1.2 The "Material Contribution" Test: The Judicial Pivot

The most significant jurisprudential development regarding AI liability in this period has been the rigorous application and expansion of the "material contribution" test. Originating from Fair Housing Council of San Fernando Valley v. Roommates.com, LLC (9th Cir. 2008), this test establishes that a platform loses immunity if it "materially contributes to the alleged unlawfulness" of the content.    

In Roommates.com, the court held that a drop-down menu requiring users to select discriminatory housing preferences was not a "neutral tool" but a developmental contribution to illegality.  Applied to GenAI in 2025 and 2026, this precedent has become the primary weapon for plaintiffs challenging the immunity of "nudify" apps and deepfake generators.   

1.2.1 AI as a Non-Neutral Tool

The defense for AI platforms has historically rested on the "Neutral Tool" doctrine—the idea that providing a general-purpose tool (like a word processor or a blank search bar) does not make the provider liable for how users utilize it.  However, the legal consensus in 2026 has shifted. Scholars and litigants now argue that an AI model designed to "undress" a photo is not a neutral tool.   

Unlike a blank text box, a "nudify" algorithm is specifically engineered to transform specific input data (a clothed photo) into specific unlawful output (NCII or CSAM).  The algorithm does not merely display the user's content; it fundamentally alters the biometric and visual data to create a new, infringing image. This functionality constitutes a "material contribution" to the development of the illegality. As noted in the Harvard Journal of Law & Technology, the "material contribution" threshold is crossed when a system provides the "technical means" to generate specific types of harm that would not exist but for the algorithm's intervention.    

1.3 The Erosion of the "Voluntariness" Defense

Platforms have historically argued that they should not be liable for user misuse of their tools, citing the "voluntariness" of the user's action in prompting the AI.  This defense posits that the "volition" to create the harm lies entirely with the user, rendering the AI a passive instrument.   

However, recent legal analysis suggests that "voluntariness" is an insufficient defense when the system's design invites or facilitates abuse. In the context of "nudify" apps, the "voluntariness" of the user in uploading a victim's photo does not absolve the platform of the "volition" inherent in the AI's processing.    

If an AI model is trained on a dataset of pornography and programmed to map nudity onto non-nude subjects, the "volition" to create the pornographic image lies with the system's architecture as much as with the user's prompt.  This aligns with the "inducement" theory of liability, where providing a tool with the specific purpose of infringing rights or violating privacy negates "neutrality."  The "voluntariness test" in this context is increasingly viewed by courts not as a shield for the platform, but as a mechanism to apportion liability between the user (who initiates) and the platform (which executes).    

Furthermore, the concept of "volition" in copyright law—which often parallels Section 230 analysis regarding "creation"—suggests that when a system's automated processes result in infringement or harm that was a foreseeable consequence of its design, the system owner possesses the requisite volition for liability.    

Part II: The Legislative Transformation (2025–2026)

While courts wrestled with doctrinal definitions, the legislative branch engaged in a decisive overhaul of the liability landscape. The enactment of the TAKE IT DOWN Act and the advancement of the DEFIANCE Act represent a shift from a "permissionless innovation" model to a "duty of care" model for AI platforms.

2.1 The TAKE IT DOWN Act of 2025

Signed into law by President Donald Trump on May 19, 2025, the "Tools to Address Known Exploitation by Immobilizing Technological Deepfakes on Websites and Networks Act" (TAKE IT DOWN Act) fundamentally alters the liability landscape for platforms hosting AI-generated adult content.    

2.1.1 Mandatory Notice-and-Removal Framework

The Act imposes an affirmative duty on "covered platforms" (defined broadly to include social media, hosting services, and AI generators) to remove "intimate visual depictions" of non-consenting adults and minors.    

The 48-Hour Rule: Upon receiving a valid removal request, a platform must remove the content and make "reasonable efforts" to identify and remove identical copies within 48 hours.  This requirement to remove "identical copies" imposes a significant technical burden, requiring platforms to employ hashing or perceptual matching technologies similar to PhotoDNA.   

Valid Request Criteria: A request must include the victim's signature, identification of the depiction (e.g., URL), and a good-faith statement of non-consent.    

2.1.2 Section 230 Exception and Enforcement

Crucially, the Act creates a statutory exception to Section 230 immunity. While Section 230(c)(1) traditionally protects platforms from being treated as the publisher, the TAKE IT DOWN Act explicitly states that a failure to comply with the notice-and-removal provisions constitutes an "unfair or deceptive act or practice" under the Federal Trade Commission Act.    

FTC Authority: The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) is empowered to enforce compliance, seeking civil penalties and injunctive relief against platforms that fail to act.  This shifts enforcement from private litigation (which can be costly and difficult for victims) to federal regulation.   

Safe Harbor: To balance the burden, the Act provides a "safe harbor" for platforms that act in good faith to remove content, protecting them from liability to the uploader (e.g., the creator of the deepfake) for wrongful removal.    

2.1.3 Criminal Liability

The Act also criminalizes the "knowing publication" of intimate visual depictions (both real and "digital forgeries") with the intent to harm, harass, or coerce.  This criminal provision applies to the individuals who generate and distribute the content, piercing the anonymity often associated with deepfake creation.   

2.2 The DEFIANCE Act (2025–2026)

While the TAKE IT DOWN Act focuses on platform removal obligations, the DEFIANCE Act (Disrupt Explicit Forged Images and Non-Consensual Edits Act) targets civil liability and victim restitution.

2.2.1 Status and Scope

As of January 13, 2026, the DEFIANCE Act passed unanimously in the Senate and moved to the House of Representatives.  The bill establishes a federal civil right of action for victims of "intimate digital forgeries."   

Liquidated Damages: Victims can sue for up to $150,000 in damages (or $250,000 if linked to sexual assault or stalking).    

Target Defendants: The Act allows suits against "creators," "distributors," and those who "knowingly possess with intent to distribute."    

2.2.2 Interaction with Section 230

The DEFIANCE Act acts as a complement to Section 230 reform. By creating a specific federal civil cause of action, it allows victims to bypass the ambiguity of state-level torts (which are often preempted by Section 230). Furthermore, because the Act targets "creators" and "distributors," plaintiffs can use the "Material Contribution" test to argue that an AI platform is a "creator" under the Act, thereby subjecting the platform to the $150,000 statutory damages per instance.    

2.3 The Role of FOSTA-SESTA in 2026

The Allow States and Victims to Fight Online Sex Trafficking Act (FOSTA-SESTA), passed in 2018, created the first major carve-out to Section 230 for platforms that "facilitate" sex trafficking.  In the 2024–2026 period, plaintiffs and regulators have attempted to expand FOSTA-SESTA's application to AI-generated content.   

2.3.1 "Synthetic" Sex Trafficking

A novel legal theory advancing in 2026 argues that the generation and distribution of realistic AI-generated CSAM or non-consensual pornography constitutes a form of "synthetic" trafficking.  By profiting from the non-consensual sexual exploitation of digital likenesses, platforms may be "knowingly assisting, supporting, or facilitating" a venture that violates sex trafficking statutes.   

While innovative, this application remains legally contested. FOSTA-SESTA was written to address the trafficking of real individuals. However, the Jane Doe v. ClothOff case (discussed below) tests whether the "appropriation of likeness" via AI falls under the umbrella of exploitation that FOSTA was meant to curb.  Critics argue that applying FOSTA-SESTA to synthetic media stretches the definition of "trafficking" too far, but proponents argue that the harm—the commodification of a person's sexual identity without consent—is identical.    

Part III: Key Case Law Analysis (2024–2026)

The judicial record between 2024 and 2026 reveals a fractured landscape where courts are increasingly willing to deny Section 230 dismissal in cases involving AI generation and biometric data, while maintaining it for traditional content moderation.

3.1 Vance v. Google LLC (N.D. Cal. Dec. 5, 2024)

Case Summary: Plaintiffs alleged that Google obtained a dataset of photographs (the "Flickr" dataset) to train its facial recognition and AI algorithms without consent, violating the Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA).    

Ruling: The U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California denied Google's motion to dismiss the BIPA claims.

Section 230 Analysis: The court found that Section 230 did not bar the privacy claims because the harm arose from Google's conduct in collecting and using biometric data to train its AI, not from the publication of third-party content.    

Significance: This ruling reinforces the "conduct vs. content" distinction. Platforms cannot use Section 230 to shield themselves from liability for the internal processes of their AI models (e.g., training on non-consensual data). This has profound implications for adult content models trained on scraped imagery without consent. The court effectively ruled that the act of training an AI is a direct action of the platform, not a hosting function.    

3.2 Ryan v. X Corp. (N.D. Cal. Dec. 9, 2024)

Case Summary: Jeremy Ryan sued X Corp. (formerly Twitter) after his accounts were suspended, alleging that X's use of AI for content moderation was flawed and tortious. He argued that the use of AI negated Section 230 immunity.    

Ruling: Judge William H. Orrick granted X Corp.'s motion to dismiss.

Reasoning: The court held that "X Corp.'s use of AI to moderate content does not negate Section 230 immunity."  Moderation decisions—whether performed by humans or algorithms—are "traditional editorial functions" protected by Section 230(c)(1).   

Key Distinction: This case distinguishes AI-as-moderator from AI-as-creator. Using AI to filter content is protected; using AI to generate content (as in ClothOff) is legally distinct and likely unprotected. This ruling provides a vital safe harbor for platforms using automated tools to comply with the TAKE IT DOWN Act's removal mandates.    

3.3 Jane Doe v. ClothOff (Filed Oct. 16, 2025, D.N.J.)

Case Summary: A federal lawsuit was filed against "ClothOff," a website offering an AI tool that "undresses" photos. A minor plaintiff ("Jane Doe") alleged that the site was used to create hyperrealistic nude images of her from a clothed social media photo.    

Claims: The suit alleges violations of:

18 U.S.C. § 2255: Civil remedies for personal injuries from CSAM offenses.

15 U.S.C. § 6851: Non-consensual distribution of intimate visual depictions (from the 2022 appropriation act).

Privacy Torts: Intrusion upon seclusion and appropriation of likeness.

Section 230 Implications: The plaintiffs argue that ClothOff is an "Information Content Provider" (ICP) because its AI created the nude imagery. The "undressing" is not user content; it is a transformative act by the platform's algorithm.

Status (Feb 2026): The case is ongoing, but it is viewed as the "first major application" of the principles underpinning the TAKE IT DOWN Act.  The argument is that Section 230 cannot protect a platform that "materially contributes" to the creation of CSAM. The outcome of this case will likely define the boundaries of the "Material Contribution" test for the next decade.    

3.4 Investigation of xAI and Grok (Jan. 2026)

Event: A coalition of 35 State Attorneys General sent a letter to xAI (Elon Musk's AI company) on January 23, 2026, regarding the Grok chatbot's generation of NCII and CSAM.    

Legal Theory: The AGs argue that Grok "promoted and facilitated the production" of harmful images through features like "spicy mode" and failed to implement adequate safeguards.

Conduct-Based Liability: The investigation focuses on consumer protection and child safety laws, which are often exempt from or operate parallel to Section 230 (under the "consistent with federal law" clause). The AGs assert that xAI's design choices—specifically the ease of generating non-consensual nudity—constitute unfair business practices.    

Significance: This represents a regulatory enforcement action rather than a civil suit, bypassing the Section 230 defense by invoking state police powers and federal criminal statute implications (CSAM). It demonstrates that even if federal civil immunity holds (which is doubtful), state regulatory power remains a potent threat.    

3.5 Liiv v. Google AI (2024-2025)

While less publicized than Vance, the Liiv proceedings and related commentary highlight the intersection of AI generation and "Unjust Enrichment." In these cases, plaintiffs argue that AI models enriched themselves by using scraped data to generate competing content. The courts have generally applied Section 230 to bar unjust enrichment claims when they are disguised defamation claims, but Vance suggests that when the claim is about the acquisition of the data (the input side), Section 230 does not apply.    

Part IV: Specific Application to AI-Generated Adult Content

The application of Section 230 to adult content in the 2024–2026 period hinges on the classification of the content (CSAM vs. NCII vs. Consensual Pornography) and the mechanism of its existence (Hosted vs. Generated).

4.1 Child Sexual Abuse Material (CSAM)

Status: No Section 230 Protection.
Section 230 has always contained an exception for federal criminal law (Section 230(e)(1)). CSAM is universally illegal under federal law (18 U.S.C. §§ 2251 et seq.).

AI Implications: The ClothOff case and the Grok investigation highlight that AI-generated images of real minors (or "identifiable" minors) constitute CSAM.    

The "Digital Forgery" Argument: Even if the image is synthetic, if it depicts a real minor in a sexualized manner, it violates the privacy and protection statutes. Courts are interpreting the creation of these images by AI as a direct violation of federal law, rendering Section 230 irrelevant.  Furthermore, the Department of Justice has signaled that it will prosecute the generation of CSAM of non-existent children under obscenity laws if the material meets the Miller test, though this remains a complex constitutional area.    

4.2 Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery (NCII) / "Revenge Porn"

Status: Conditional Protection Removed by TAKE IT DOWN Act.
Prior to 2025, platforms often used Section 230 to avoid liability for hosting revenge porn, arguing they were neutral hosts.

Post-2025 Regime: The TAKE IT DOWN Act creates a specific mechanism that overrides Section 230. If a platform fails to remove notified NCII within 48 hours, it loses its immunity regarding that content and faces FTC penalties.    

Deepfakes: The definition of "intimate visual depiction" in the Act explicitly includes "digital forgeries" (deepfakes). Therefore, platforms hosting AI-generated deepfake pornography of adults are subject to the same strict takedown requirements as those hosting authentic revenge porn.    

4.3 The "Nudify" App Ecosystem

Apps and sites that exist solely or primarily to generate nude images from clothed photos (like ClothOff or deepfake bots on Telegram) face the highest liability risk.

Information Content Provider (ICP): These platforms fail the "neutral tool" test. Their value proposition is the transformation of content. Under the Roommates.com precedent, they are the "developers" of the illicit content.

Product Liability: As noted in the Brown Political Review (2026), litigation is shifting toward product liability. If an AI tool is "defective" because it harms users (or third parties) through its design, Section 230 (which protects publishers) may not apply to product defects.  The "failure to warn" or "negligent design" claims (e.g., releasing a model without safety filters) are gaining traction as viable legal strategies that bypass Section 230 entirely.    

Part V: State-Level Interventions and Federal Preemption

While the federal government enacted the TAKE IT DOWN Act, states have been aggressive in legislating against AI-generated adult content, creating a patchwork of liability that interacts complexly with Section 230.

5.1 The Role of State Attorneys General

As seen in the xAI/Grok letter, State AGs are utilizing consumer protection laws to police AI.

Unfair and Deceptive Acts: AGs argue that marketing an AI tool as "safe" while it generates CSAM is deceptive.

Public Nuisance: Some states are exploring public nuisance theories to hold platforms accountable for the societal harm of mass-produced NCII.    

Preemption: Section 230 generally preempts state civil claims. However, it does not preempt state criminal laws (that are consistent with Section 230) or federal criminal laws. States are increasingly codifying NCII and deepfake creation as criminal offenses to bypass the Section 230 civil immunity shield.    

5.2 Constitutional Challenges

Attempts by states to regulate "political deepfakes" or broad categories of AI content have faced First Amendment challenges.

California's Defeat: A federal judge struck down a California law blocking deceptive political deepfakes, ruling it was not narrowly tailored.    

Relevance to Adult Content: While political speech is highly protected, obscenity and CSAM are not. However, "virtual" pornography of non-existent adults remains a constitutionally protected category (under Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition). The legal battleground in 2026 is distinguishing between "harmful deepfakes of real people" (unprotected/actionable) and "generative erotica of fictional characters" (protected).  The Jane Doe v. ClothOff case specifically argues that AI-generated CSAM involving identifiable minors does not receive First Amendment protection.

Part VII: Future Outlook and Strategic Implications
7.1 The End of "Passive" Immunity

The era of the "passive host" is effectively over for entities dealing with Generative AI. By 2026, any platform deploying generative tools must treat itself as a potential creator. The legal advice for such platforms has shifted from "do not moderate to avoid knowledge" (a pre-230 fallacy, but often still a strategic choice) to "moderate aggressively and comply with takedown requests immediately" to secure the safe harbors of the TAKE IT DOWN Act.    

7.2 The Rise of Duty of Care

The legislative trend, exemplified by the TAKE IT DOWN Act and the proposed KOSA (Kids Online Safety Act), is moving toward a "Duty of Care" model. This model posits that platforms have an affirmative duty to design their products safely. For AI, this means "safety by design"—training models to refuse prompts for NCII and CSAM before the content is generated, rather than relying on removal after the fact.    

7.3 The "Black Box" Liability Gap

A lingering challenge is the "Black Box" phenomenon. If a decentralized, open-source AI model (hosted on decentralized infrastructure) generates NCII, who is the "provider" under the TAKE IT DOWN Act?  Future litigation will likely target the "infrastructure" layer—cloud providers and payment processors—as identified in the MultiState 2026 outlook.    

7.4 Global Harmonization vs. Fragmentation

The U.S. shift towards liability brings it closer to the European Union's AI Act, which classifies generative AI as high-risk and imposes strict transparency and safety obligations.  However, fragmentation remains. While the EU focuses on ex ante regulation (preventing the harm), the U.S. framework (TAKE IT DOWN, DEFIANCE) focuses on ex post remedy (punishing the harm). The Jane Doe v. ClothOff case will likely serve as the bellwether for whether the U.S. judiciary will bridge this gap by interpreting Section 230 to require ex ante safety measures (like age verification and content filters) as a condition of immunity.    

Conclusion

Between 2024 and 2026, the application of Section 230 to AI-generated adult content underwent a radical contraction. While the statute continues to protect platforms in their capacity as neutral hosts of user speech (as affirmed in Ryan v. X Corp.), it has been pierced by the "Material Contribution" test regarding generative outputs. The enactment of the TAKE IT DOWN Act codified a new liability regime for non-consensual intimate imagery, explicitly overriding Section 230 immunity for non-compliant platforms.

For platforms hosting AI-generated content, the legal shield is no longer automatic. It is conditional, conduct-dependent, and increasingly porous. The defining legal principle of 2026 is that an algorithm which transforms, undresses, or hallucinates is not a "Good Samaritan" host—it is an author, and as such, it must bear the burden of its creations. The convergence of the "Material Contribution" judicial doctrine and the "Take It Down" legislative mandate signals the end of the internet's "Wild West" era for synthetic media, ushering in a new age of algorithmic accountability.

Glossary of Key Terms

Interactive Computer Service (ICS): Any system that provides access to the internet (e.g., ISP, social media). Traditionally protected by Section 230.    

Information Content Provider (ICP): Any person or entity responsible for the creation or development of information. Not protected by Section 230.    

Material Contribution Test: A legal test determining if a platform's tools or design contributed to the illegality of the content, thereby making it an ICP.    

NCII: Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery (often called "revenge porn").

CSAM: Child Sexual Abuse Material.

Digital Forgery: A term defined in the TAKE IT DOWN Act referring to highly realistic AI-generated depictions of individuals.    

RLHF: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback—a training method for AI that plaintiffs argue constitutes "editorial" and "creative" involvement by the developer.    

Voluntariness Test: A legal doctrine assessing whether a user's action was free from coercion; in AI contexts, it examines whether the user's prompt or the AI's design was the primary driver of the content.

The Algorithmic Author: Section 230, Generative AI, and the Legal Crisis of Synthetic Adult Content (2024–2026)
Executive Summary

The period between 2024 and early 2026 represents the most significant upheaval in internet jurisprudence since the enactment of the Communications Decency Act (CDA) in 1996. For thirty years, Section 230 of the CDA served as the foundational immunity shield for the internet economy, protecting "interactive computer services" from liability for content created by third parties. This regime, predicated on the distinction between a passive host and an active publisher, has been fundamentally destabilized by the advent of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI).

This report provides an exhaustive analysis of the application of Section 230 to AI-generated adult content—specifically Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery (NCII) and Child Sexual Abuse Material (CSAM)—during this critical two-year window. It argues that the proliferation of "nudify" applications, deepfake generators, and Large Language Models (LLMs) capable of producing illicit imagery has forced a judicial and legislative reckoning. The "intermediary" logic of the 1990s is collapsing under the weight of "algorithmic volition," where software does not merely transmit user speech but actively constructs it.

The analysis details the transition from the "Neutral Tool" doctrine to the "Material Contribution" test, examining how courts in 2025 and 2026 have begun to classify AI models as "Information Content Providers" (ICPs), thereby stripping them of Section 230 immunity. It provides in-depth scrutiny of landmark litigation, including Vance v. Google (2024), Ryan v. X Corp. (2024), and Jane Doe v. ClothOff (2025), and analyzes the transformative impact of the TAKE IT DOWN Act of 2025, which codified a federal exception to Section 230 for non-compliant platforms.

Part I: The Doctrinal Crisis of Section 230 in the Age of AI
1.1 The Statutory Foundation and the "Categorical Error"

To understand the crisis of 2024–2026, one must first analyze the statutory architecture that governed the pre-AI internet. Section 230(c)(1) of the Communications Decency Act provides that "No provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider."  This "twenty-six words" created a bifurcated legal reality: the "Interactive Computer Service" (ICS), which provides the infrastructure, and the "Information Content Provider" (ICP), which provides the content.   

For decades, courts interpreted this broadly. In Zeran v. America Online (1997), the Fourth Circuit ruled that holding a service provider liable for its exercise of traditional editorial functions—such as deciding whether to publish, withdraw, postpone, or alter content—would inherently treat it as a publisher, violating the statute.  This created a regime where platforms were incentivized to moderate voluntarily (the "Good Samaritan" provision of Section 230(c)(2)) but were not penalized for failing to do so.   

However, the integration of Generative AI into online platforms has introduced a "categorical error" in applying these protections. Legal scholars and recent filings argue that GenAI systems defy the ICS/ICP dichotomy. An AI model does not merely "host" information provided by a user; it "hallucinates," synthesizes, and renders pixels that did not previously exist.    

When a user prompts an AI to "create a nude image of [Person X]," the user provides the intent, but the AI provides the expression. The resulting image is a composite of the model's training data (latent space) and its algorithmic weights. Because the "creation" of information is the defining characteristic of an Information Content Provider under Section 230(f)(3), the AI system itself—and by extension, its developer—becomes the ICP.  As argued in the Brown Political Review (2026), treating LLMs as passive intermediaries ignores the technical reality that their outputs are products of internal datasets and learning algorithms. Any harmful statement or image is a result of the system's training and design choices, not merely the user's prompt.    

1.2 The "Material Contribution" Test: The Judicial Pivot

The most significant jurisprudential development regarding AI liability in this period has been the rigorous application and expansion of the "material contribution" test. Originating from Fair Housing Council of San Fernando Valley v. Roommates.com, LLC (9th Cir. 2008), this test establishes that a platform loses immunity if it "materially contributes to the alleged unlawfulness" of the content.    

In Roommates.com, the court held that a drop-down menu requiring users to select discriminatory housing preferences was not a "neutral tool" but a developmental contribution to illegality.  Applied to GenAI in 2025 and 2026, this precedent has become the primary weapon for plaintiffs challenging the immunity of "nudify" apps and deepfake generators.   

1.2.1 AI as a Non-Neutral Tool

The defense for AI platforms has historically rested on the "Neutral Tool" doctrine—the idea that providing a general-purpose tool (like a word processor or a blank search bar) does not make the provider liable for how users utilize it.  However, the legal consensus in 2026 has shifted. Scholars and litigants now argue that an AI model designed to "undress" a photo is not a neutral tool.   

Unlike a blank text box, a "nudify" algorithm is specifically engineered to transform specific input data (a clothed photo) into specific unlawful output (NCII or CSAM).  The algorithm does not merely display the user's content; it fundamentally alters the biometric and visual data to create a new, infringing image. This functionality constitutes a "material contribution" to the development of the illegality. As noted in the Harvard Journal of Law & Technology, the "material contribution" threshold is crossed when a system provides the "technical means" to generate specific types of harm that would not exist but for the algorithm's intervention.    

1.3 The Erosion of the "Voluntariness" Defense

Platforms have historically argued that they should not be liable for user misuse of their tools, citing the "voluntariness" of the user's action in prompting the AI.  This defense posits that the "volition" to create the harm lies entirely with the user, rendering the AI a passive instrument.   

However, recent legal analysis suggests that "voluntariness" is an insufficient defense when the system's design invites or facilitates abuse. In the context of "nudify" apps, the "voluntariness" of the user in uploading a victim's photo does not absolve the platform of the "volition" inherent in the AI's processing.    

If an AI model is trained on a dataset of pornography and programmed to map nudity onto non-nude subjects, the "volition" to create the pornographic image lies with the system's architecture as much as with the user's prompt.  This aligns with the "inducement" theory of liability, where providing a tool with the specific purpose of infringing rights or violating privacy negates "neutrality."  The "voluntariness test" in this context is increasingly viewed by courts not as a shield for the platform, but as a mechanism to apportion liability between the user (who initiates) and the platform (which executes).    

Furthermore, the concept of "volition" in copyright law—which often parallels Section 230 analysis regarding "creation"—suggests that when a system's automated processes result in infringement or harm that was a foreseeable consequence of its design, the system owner possesses the requisite volition for liability.    

Part II: The Legislative Transformation (2025–2026)

While courts wrestled with doctrinal definitions, the legislative branch engaged in a decisive overhaul of the liability landscape. The enactment of the TAKE IT DOWN Act and the advancement of the DEFIANCE Act represent a shift from a "permissionless innovation" model to a "duty of care" model for AI platforms.

2.1 The TAKE IT DOWN Act of 2025

Signed into law by President Donald Trump on May 19, 2025, the "Tools to Address Known Exploitation by Immobilizing Technological Deepfakes on Websites and Networks Act" (TAKE IT DOWN Act) fundamentally alters the liability landscape for platforms hosting AI-generated adult content.    

2.1.1 Mandatory Notice-and-Removal Framework

The Act imposes an affirmative duty on "covered platforms" (defined broadly to include social media, hosting services, and AI generators) to remove "intimate visual depictions" of non-consenting adults and minors.    

The 48-Hour Rule: Upon receiving a valid removal request, a platform must remove the content and make "reasonable efforts" to identify and remove identical copies within 48 hours.  This requirement to remove "identical copies" imposes a significant technical burden, requiring platforms to employ hashing or perceptual matching technologies similar to PhotoDNA.   

Valid Request Criteria: A request must include the victim's signature, identification of the depiction (e.g., URL), and a good-faith statement of non-consent.    

2.1.2 Section 230 Exception and Enforcement

Crucially, the Act creates a statutory exception to Section 230 immunity. While Section 230(c)(1) traditionally protects platforms from being treated as the publisher, the TAKE IT DOWN Act explicitly states that a failure to comply with the notice-and-removal provisions constitutes an "unfair or deceptive act or practice" under the Federal Trade Commission Act.    

FTC Authority: The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) is empowered to enforce compliance, seeking civil penalties and injunctive relief against platforms that fail to act.  This shifts enforcement from private litigation (which can be costly and difficult for victims) to federal regulation.   

Safe Harbor: To balance the burden, the Act provides a "safe harbor" for platforms that act in good faith to remove content, protecting them from liability to the uploader (e.g., the creator of the deepfake) for wrongful removal.    

2.1.3 Criminal Liability

The Act also criminalizes the "knowing publication" of intimate visual depictions (both real and "digital forgeries") with the intent to harm, harass, or coerce.  This criminal provision applies to the individuals who generate and distribute the content, piercing the anonymity often associated with deepfake creation.   

2.2 The DEFIANCE Act (2025–2026)

While the TAKE IT DOWN Act focuses on platform removal obligations, the DEFIANCE Act (Disrupt Explicit Forged Images and Non-Consensual Edits Act) targets civil liability and victim restitution.

2.2.1 Status and Scope

As of January 13, 2026, the DEFIANCE Act passed unanimously in the Senate and moved to the House of Representatives.  The bill establishes a federal civil right of action for victims of "intimate digital forgeries."   

Liquidated Damages: Victims can sue for up to $150,000 in damages (or $250,000 if linked to sexual assault or stalking).    

Target Defendants: The Act allows suits against "creators," "distributors," and those who "knowingly possess with intent to distribute."    

2.2.2 Interaction with Section 230

The DEFIANCE Act acts as a complement to Section 230 reform. By creating a specific federal civil cause of action, it allows victims to bypass the ambiguity of state-level torts (which are often preempted by Section 230). Furthermore, because the Act targets "creators" and "distributors," plaintiffs can use the "Material Contribution" test to argue that an AI platform is a "creator" under the Act, thereby subjecting the platform to the $150,000 statutory damages per instance.    

2.3 The Role of FOSTA-SESTA in 2026

The Allow States and Victims to Fight Online Sex Trafficking Act (FOSTA-SESTA), passed in 2018, created the first major carve-out to Section 230 for platforms that "facilitate" sex trafficking.  In the 2024–2026 period, plaintiffs and regulators have attempted to expand FOSTA-SESTA's application to AI-generated content.   

2.3.1 "Synthetic" Sex Trafficking

A novel legal theory advancing in 2026 argues that the generation and distribution of realistic AI-generated CSAM or non-consensual pornography constitutes a form of "synthetic" trafficking.  By profiting from the non-consensual sexual exploitation of digital likenesses, platforms may be "knowingly assisting, supporting, or facilitating" a venture that violates sex trafficking statutes.   

While innovative, this application remains legally contested. FOSTA-SESTA was written to address the trafficking of real individuals. However, the Jane Doe v. ClothOff case (discussed below) tests whether the "appropriation of likeness" via AI falls under the umbrella of exploitation that FOSTA was meant to curb.  Critics argue that applying FOSTA-SESTA to synthetic media stretches the definition of "trafficking" too far, but proponents argue that the harm—the commodification of a person's sexual identity without consent—is identical.    

Part III: Key Case Law Analysis (2024–2026)

The judicial record between 2024 and 2026 reveals a fractured landscape where courts are increasingly willing to deny Section 230 dismissal in cases involving AI generation and biometric data, while maintaining it for traditional content moderation.

3.1 Vance v. Google LLC (N.D. Cal. Dec. 5, 2024)

Case Summary: Plaintiffs alleged that Google obtained a dataset of photographs (the "Flickr" dataset) to train its facial recognition and AI algorithms without consent, violating the Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA).    

Ruling: The U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California denied Google's motion to dismiss the BIPA claims.

Section 230 Analysis: The court found that Section 230 did not bar the privacy claims because the harm arose from Google's conduct in collecting and using biometric data to train its AI, not from the publication of third-party content.    

Significance: This ruling reinforces the "conduct vs. content" distinction. Platforms cannot use Section 230 to shield themselves from liability for the internal processes of their AI models (e.g., training on non-consensual data). This has profound implications for adult content models trained on scraped imagery without consent. The court effectively ruled that the act of training an AI is a direct action of the platform, not a hosting function.    

3.2 Ryan v. X Corp. (N.D. Cal. Dec. 9, 2024)

Case Summary: Jeremy Ryan sued X Corp. (formerly Twitter) after his accounts were suspended, alleging that X's use of AI for content moderation was flawed and tortious. He argued that the use of AI negated Section 230 immunity.    

Ruling: Judge William H. Orrick granted X Corp.'s motion to dismiss.

Reasoning: The court held that "X Corp.'s use of AI to moderate content does not negate Section 230 immunity."  Moderation decisions—whether performed by humans or algorithms—are "traditional editorial functions" protected by Section 230(c)(1).   

Key Distinction: This case distinguishes AI-as-moderator from AI-as-creator. Using AI to filter content is protected; using AI to generate content (as in ClothOff) is legally distinct and likely unprotected. This ruling provides a vital safe harbor for platforms using automated tools to comply with the TAKE IT DOWN Act's removal mandates.    

3.3 Jane Doe v. ClothOff (Filed Oct. 16, 2025, D.N.J.)

Case Summary: A federal lawsuit was filed against "ClothOff," a website offering an AI tool that "undresses" photos. A minor plaintiff ("Jane Doe") alleged that the site was used to create hyperrealistic nude images of her from a clothed social media photo.    

Claims: The suit alleges violations of:

18 U.S.C. § 2255: Civil remedies for personal injuries from CSAM offenses.

15 U.S.C. § 6851: Non-consensual distribution of intimate visual depictions (from the 2022 appropriation act).

Privacy Torts: Intrusion upon seclusion and appropriation of likeness.

Section 230 Implications: The plaintiffs argue that ClothOff is an "Information Content Provider" (ICP) because its AI created the nude imagery. The "undressing" is not user content; it is a transformative act by the platform's algorithm.

Status (Feb 2026): The case is ongoing, but it is viewed as the "first major application" of the principles underpinning the TAKE IT DOWN Act.  The argument is that Section 230 cannot protect a platform that "materially contributes" to the creation of CSAM. The outcome of this case will likely define the boundaries of the "Material Contribution" test for the next decade.    

3.4 Investigation of xAI and Grok (Jan. 2026)

Event: A coalition of 35 State Attorneys General sent a letter to xAI (Elon Musk's AI company) on January 23, 2026, regarding the Grok chatbot's generation of NCII and CSAM.    

Legal Theory: The AGs argue that Grok "promoted and facilitated the production" of harmful images through features like "spicy mode" and failed to implement adequate safeguards.

Conduct-Based Liability: The investigation focuses on consumer protection and child safety laws, which are often exempt from or operate parallel to Section 230 (under the "consistent with federal law" clause). The AGs assert that xAI's design choices—specifically the ease of generating non-consensual nudity—constitute unfair business practices.    

Significance: This represents a regulatory enforcement action rather than a civil suit, bypassing the Section 230 defense by invoking state police powers and federal criminal statute implications (CSAM). It demonstrates that even if federal civil immunity holds (which is doubtful), state regulatory power remains a potent threat.    

3.5 Liiv v. Google AI (2024-2025)

While less publicized than Vance, the Liiv proceedings and related commentary highlight the intersection of AI generation and "Unjust Enrichment." In these cases, plaintiffs argue that AI models enriched themselves by using scraped data to generate competing content. The courts have generally applied Section 230 to bar unjust enrichment claims when they are disguised defamation claims, but Vance suggests that when the claim is about the acquisition of the data (the input side), Section 230 does not apply.    

Part IV: Specific Application to AI-Generated Adult Content

The application of Section 230 to adult content in the 2024–2026 period hinges on the classification of the content (CSAM vs. NCII vs. Consensual Pornography) and the mechanism of its existence (Hosted vs. Generated).

4.1 Child Sexual Abuse Material (CSAM)

Status: No Section 230 Protection.
Section 230 has always contained an exception for federal criminal law (Section 230(e)(1)). CSAM is universally illegal under federal law (18 U.S.C. §§ 2251 et seq.).

AI Implications: The ClothOff case and the Grok investigation highlight that AI-generated images of real minors (or "identifiable" minors) constitute CSAM.    

The "Digital Forgery" Argument: Even if the image is synthetic, if it depicts a real minor in a sexualized manner, it violates the privacy and protection statutes. Courts are interpreting the creation of these images by AI as a direct violation of federal law, rendering Section 230 irrelevant.  Furthermore, the Department of Justice has signaled that it will prosecute the generation of CSAM of non-existent children under obscenity laws if the material meets the Miller test, though this remains a complex constitutional area.    

4.2 Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery (NCII) / "Revenge Porn"

Status: Conditional Protection Removed by TAKE IT DOWN Act.
Prior to 2025, platforms often used Section 230 to avoid liability for hosting revenge porn, arguing they were neutral hosts.

Post-2025 Regime: The TAKE IT DOWN Act creates a specific mechanism that overrides Section 230. If a platform fails to remove notified NCII within 48 hours, it loses its immunity regarding that content and faces FTC penalties.    

Deepfakes: The definition of "intimate visual depiction" in the Act explicitly includes "digital forgeries" (deepfakes). Therefore, platforms hosting AI-generated deepfake pornography of adults are subject to the same strict takedown requirements as those hosting authentic revenge porn.    

4.3 The "Nudify" App Ecosystem

Apps and sites that exist solely or primarily to generate nude images from clothed photos (like ClothOff or deepfake bots on Telegram) face the highest liability risk.

Information Content Provider (ICP): These platforms fail the "neutral tool" test. Their value proposition is the transformation of content. Under the Roommates.com precedent, they are the "developers" of the illicit content.

Product Liability: As noted in the Brown Political Review (2026), litigation is shifting toward product liability. If an AI tool is "defective" because it harms users (or third parties) through its design, Section 230 (which protects publishers) may not apply to product defects.  The "failure to warn" or "negligent design" claims (e.g., releasing a model without safety filters) are gaining traction as viable legal strategies that bypass Section 230 entirely.    

Part V: State-Level Interventions and Federal Preemption

While the federal government enacted the TAKE IT DOWN Act, states have been aggressive in legislating against AI-generated adult content, creating a patchwork of liability that interacts complexly with Section 230.

5.1 The Role of State Attorneys General

As seen in the xAI/Grok letter, State AGs are utilizing consumer protection laws to police AI.

Unfair and Deceptive Acts: AGs argue that marketing an AI tool as "safe" while it generates CSAM is deceptive.

Public Nuisance: Some states are exploring public nuisance theories to hold platforms accountable for the societal harm of mass-produced NCII.    

Preemption: Section 230 generally preempts state civil claims. However, it does not preempt state criminal laws (that are consistent with Section 230) or federal criminal laws. States are increasingly codifying NCII and deepfake creation as criminal offenses to bypass the Section 230 civil immunity shield.    

5.2 Constitutional Challenges

Attempts by states to regulate "political deepfakes" or broad categories of AI content have faced First Amendment challenges.

California's Defeat: A federal judge struck down a California law blocking deceptive political deepfakes, ruling it was not narrowly tailored.    

Relevance to Adult Content: While political speech is highly protected, obscenity and CSAM are not. However, "virtual" pornography of non-existent adults remains a constitutionally protected category (under Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition). The legal battleground in 2026 is distinguishing between "harmful deepfakes of real people" (unprotected/actionable) and "generative erotica of fictional characters" (protected).  The Jane Doe v. ClothOff case specifically argues that AI-generated CSAM involving identifiable minors does not receive First Amendment protection.

Part VII: Future Outlook and Strategic Implications
7.1 The End of "Passive" Immunity

The era of the "passive host" is effectively over for entities dealing with Generative AI. By 2026, any platform deploying generative tools must treat itself as a potential creator. The legal advice for such platforms has shifted from "do not moderate to avoid knowledge" (a pre-230 fallacy, but often still a strategic choice) to "moderate aggressively and comply with takedown requests immediately" to secure the safe harbors of the TAKE IT DOWN Act.    

7.2 The Rise of Duty of Care

The legislative trend, exemplified by the TAKE IT DOWN Act and the proposed KOSA (Kids Online Safety Act), is moving toward a "Duty of Care" model. This model posits that platforms have an affirmative duty to design their products safely. For AI, this means "safety by design"—training models to refuse prompts for NCII and CSAM before the content is generated, rather than relying on removal after the fact.    

7.3 The "Black Box" Liability Gap

A lingering challenge is the "Black Box" phenomenon. If a decentralized, open-source AI model (hosted on decentralized infrastructure) generates NCII, who is the "provider" under the TAKE IT DOWN Act?  Future litigation will likely target the "infrastructure" layer—cloud providers and payment processors—as identified in the MultiState 2026 outlook.    

7.4 Global Harmonization vs. Fragmentation

The U.S. shift towards liability brings it closer to the European Union's AI Act, which classifies generative AI as high-risk and imposes strict transparency and safety obligations.  However, fragmentation remains. While the EU focuses on ex ante regulation (preventing the harm), the U.S. framework (TAKE IT DOWN, DEFIANCE) focuses on ex post remedy (punishing the harm). The Jane Doe v. ClothOff case will likely serve as the bellwether for whether the U.S. judiciary will bridge this gap by interpreting Section 230 to require ex ante safety measures (like age verification and content filters) as a condition of immunity.    

Conclusion

Between 2024 and 2026, the application of Section 230 to AI-generated adult content underwent a radical contraction. While the statute continues to protect platforms in their capacity as neutral hosts of user speech (as affirmed in Ryan v. X Corp.), it has been pierced by the "Material Contribution" test regarding generative outputs. The enactment of the TAKE IT DOWN Act codified a new liability regime for non-consensual intimate imagery, explicitly overriding Section 230 immunity for non-compliant platforms.

For platforms hosting AI-generated content, the legal shield is no longer automatic. It is conditional, conduct-dependent, and increasingly porous. The defining legal principle of 2026 is that an algorithm which transforms, undresses, or hallucinates is not a "Good Samaritan" host—it is an author, and as such, it must bear the burden of its creations. The convergence of the "Material Contribution" judicial doctrine and the "Take It Down" legislative mandate signals the end of the internet's "Wild West" era for synthetic media, ushering in a new age of algorithmic accountability.

Glossary of Key Terms

Interactive Computer Service (ICS): Any system that provides access to the internet (e.g., ISP, social media). Traditionally protected by Section 230.    

Information Content Provider (ICP): Any person or entity responsible for the creation or development of information. Not protected by Section 230.    

Material Contribution Test: A legal test determining if a platform's tools or design contributed to the illegality of the content, thereby making it an ICP.    

NCII: Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery (often called "revenge porn").

CSAM: Child Sexual Abuse Material.

Digital Forgery: A term defined in the TAKE IT DOWN Act referring to highly realistic AI-generated depictions of individuals.    

RLHF: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback—a training method for AI that plaintiffs argue constitutes "editorial" and "creative" involvement by the developer.    

Voluntariness Test: A legal doctrine assessing whether a user's action was free from coercion; in AI contexts, it examines whether the user's prompt or the AI's design was the primary driver of the content.
